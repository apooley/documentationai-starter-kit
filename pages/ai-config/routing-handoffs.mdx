## Understand routing and handoffs

Routing controls which assistant or playbook handles an incoming interaction, and how conversations move between AI and humans over time.

Use routing to:

- Direct different channels to different assistants.
- Match specific intents or topics to specialized playbooks.
- Treat high‑value customers differently from general traffic.
- Define when AI should escalate to a human and when humans can send work back to AI.

Handoffs define how ownership of a conversation changes between AI and human operators. A handoff can be:

- **AI → human**: when the model is unsure, out of scope, or a policy requires a person.
- **Human → AI**: when an operator wants the assistant to draft replies or continue automation.
- **AI → AI**: when one assistant or playbook reaches its goal and another configuration is better suited for the next step.

<Callout kind="info">

You need tenant admin or equivalent configuration permissions to edit routing logic and handoff rules. Coordinate with operations and support leads before changing production behavior.

</Callout>

## Key concepts

Routing in Autoch.at builds on the same ideas as email and SMS workflows: each path defines a goal, a priority, and a series of steps from intake through resolution.

### Sources and channels

Sources describe **where** a conversation starts:

- Email, SMS, or chat widgets.
- Integrations that create conversations from external systems.
- Internal tools that open a new thread on behalf of a customer.

Configure routing so each source and channel goes to the right starting assistant or playbook.

### Assistants and playbooks

Assistants are reusable AI configurations that bundle instructions, tools, and context. Playbooks add structured workflows and goals on top of those assistants.

Routing rules decide:

- Which assistant should own a new conversation.
- Which playbook runs first for a given type of request.
- When to move between assistants or playbooks as the conversation evolves.

Link this page with:

- [Assistants](/pages/ai-config/assistants) to standardize AI behavior and create variants.
- [Playbooks](/pages/ai-config/playbooks) to define step‑by‑step automation.

### Conditions and priorities

Routing conditions mirror the workflow matching behavior in email and SMS:

- Match on **channel**, **sender**, **subject/content patterns**, or **metadata** like customer segment.
- Assign **priorities** so more specific rules override general defaults.
- Always keep a **fallback route** for traffic that does not match any explicit path.

Use test tools where available (similar to email workflow matching) to verify how an example message or conversation will be routed before you launch changes.

### Handoff triggers

Common triggers for a handoff include:

- Model confidence falls below a threshold.
- The user asks for a human or mentions a regulated topic.
- A playbook step reaches a goal and needs a different assistant to continue.
- A human operator clicks a control to take over, request a draft, or send back to AI.

Treat each trigger as a condition you can tune and monitor rather than a one‑time switch.

## Configure a safe routing and handoff workflow

This workflow walks through designing, configuring, and validating routing and handoffs before they affect real customers.

<Steps>

<Step title="Map your routing and handoff goals" icon="settings">

Start by writing down how you want conversations to flow across channels and teams.

- List your primary channels (email, SMS, chat) and where you want each to land first.
- Identify specialized topics or intents that need dedicated assistants or playbooks.
- Decide when AI must escalate to humans by policy (for example, billing disputes or account closure).
- Capture where humans should be able to send work back to AI, like drafting long explanations or following a structured playbook.

You should end this step with a simple diagram or bullet list of your desired paths, including at least one default route and one escalation path.

</Step>

<Step title="Prepare assistants and playbooks" icon="zap">

Create or refine the assistants and playbooks that routing will target.

- Use the **Assistants** configuration to define behavior, tools, and tone for each role (for example, general support, billing, technical).
- Create variants for experimentation, such as a new assistant version you want to test on a subset of traffic.
- Configure **Playbooks** for repeatable flows like onboarding, FAQ deflection, or triage steps.

Confirm that each assistant and playbook behaves as expected in isolation before wiring them into routing rules.

</Step>

<Step title="Define entry routing by channel" icon="mail">

Set up how new conversations are routed from each entry point.

- For each email, SMS, or chat source, assign a default assistant or playbook that should own new conversations.
- Apply conditions to override the default when you detect specific patterns (for example, subject words, keywords in the first message, or custom tags).
- Align priorities so the most specific, high‑impact routes execute first, with a clear fallback for unmatched traffic.

Verify that every entry route links to a real, active assistant or playbook and that you have no overlapping rules with the same priority.

</Step>

<Step title="Add intent and segment-based routing" icon="users">

Layer additional routing rules that refine who handles which requests.

- Use model‑identified intents or content patterns to route specialized topics to focused assistants.
- Treat high‑value or at‑risk customer segments differently by routing them directly to dedicated playbooks or faster human queues.
- Keep a generalist assistant or playbook as a safety net for unexpected topics.

Test a few representative examples for each intent or segment to confirm that traffic lands where you expect.

</Step>

<Step title="Configure AI to human handoffs" icon="users">

Create clear, predictable paths from AI to human operators.

- Define thresholds for when the model should offer or force escalation (for example, repeated misunderstandings, policy topics, or explicit user requests).
- Specify which queues or teams should receive escalated conversations from each assistant or playbook.
- Include guidance for operators on what context the AI will provide and what they should do first when they take over.

A successful handoff preserves the conversation history and clearly marks why the AI escalated, so humans can act quickly without guessing.

</Step>

<Step title="Configure human to AI collaboration" icon="terminal">

Give operators safe ways to leverage AI without losing control.

- Enable controls that let humans request AI‑generated drafts while they remain the final approver.
- Allow operators to send routine or well‑defined follow‑up steps back into a playbook once they confirm the situation is safe for automation.
- Prevent loops by defining when AI must not hand back to humans automatically (for example, after a human has already made a final decision).

Train operators on how and when to use these tools, and include expectations around reviewing and editing AI suggestions before sending.

</Step>

<Step title="Test with non-production traffic" icon="play-circle">

Before routing real customers, test your configuration on controlled or low‑risk traffic.

- Use internal test accounts, seed email addresses, or training numbers to generate sample conversations.
- Exercise each routing rule and handoff path, including edge cases like ambiguous intents and escalation triggers.
- Validate that the right assistant or playbook is selected, and that handoffs preserve full context and metadata.

Your routing is ready for limited rollout when tests demonstrate consistent, predictable flows across all mapped scenarios.

</Step>

<Step title="Roll out gradually and monitor" icon="bar-chart">

Launch routing and handoffs in stages so you can respond quickly if behavior does not match expectations.

- Start with a small percentage of traffic or a limited set of channels or segments.
- Monitor conversation outcomes, escalation rates, and operator feedback.
- Adjust conditions, priorities, and thresholds based on real‑world data, then expand coverage incrementally.

Keep change logs and communicate updates with support and operations teams so they understand how routing and handoffs are evolving.

</Step>

</Steps>

## Common routing strategies

Different teams organize routing around different anchors. Use these strategies as starting points and combine them where it makes sense.

<Tabs>

<Tab title="By channel" icon="mail">

Routing by channel focuses on **where** the conversation originates.

Use this strategy when:

- Teams are organized by channel (email support vs SMS vs live chat).
- You want different assistants or playbooks for synchronous vs asynchronous conversations.
- Some channels are more sensitive and need stricter human review.

Typical setup:

- Route all email from support addresses to a general support assistant plus playbook.
- Send SMS from shortcodes to a concise, mobile‑optimized assistant.
- Direct web chat to a faster‑responding assistant with tighter escalation thresholds.

Combine channel‑based routing with priorities so channel defaults do not override more specific rules like high‑value customers or sensitive intents.

</Tab>

<Tab title="By intent or topic" icon="code">

Intent‑based routing focuses on **what** the customer is asking for.

Use this strategy when:

- You have clear categories like billing, technical issues, onboarding, or cancellations.
- Specialized assistants or playbooks handle each category better than a single generalist.
- You want to measure performance per intent (deflection, CSAT, resolution time).

Typical setup:

- Use early messages to detect intent (from subject lines, first questions, or keywords).
- Route billing intents to a billing assistant with access to relevant context and policies.
- Route technical intents to a troubleshooting playbook with guided steps and tool use.
- Keep a fallback intent that routes to a general assistant or human triage queue.

As your library of intents grows, revisit priorities and coverage to avoid gaps or overlaps across categories.

</Tab>

<Tab title="By customer segment" icon="users">

Segment‑based routing focuses on **who** is contacting you.

Use this strategy when:

- You distinguish between tiers like enterprise, premium, and standard customers.
- Certain customers need faster responses or more experienced operators.
- You use different policies or SLAs for different groups.

Typical setup:

- Enrich conversations with CRM data to tag each contact with a segment.
- Route high‑value or high‑risk segments directly to specialized assistants plus lower thresholds for human handoff.
- Use more automation for low‑risk, high‑volume segments by keeping them in playbooks longer before escalation.

Always keep a visible path for any segment to reach a human when required, regardless of automation level or tier.

</Tab>

</Tabs>

## Operate and iterate safely

Once routing and handoffs are live, treat them as an operational system rather than a one‑time configuration.

- **Review metrics regularly**: Track deflection, escalation rates, handle times, and customer satisfaction by channel, intent, and segment.
- **Run structured experiments**: Use assistant variants and playbook changes to trial new behaviors on limited traffic before making them defaults.
- **Align with training data**: Update your training context and examples when you add new routing paths or change policies, so the AI understands the new expectations.
- **Coordinate with workflows**: Make sure email and SMS workflows still behave as intended when you add or modify AI‑driven routing rules.

<Callout kind="info">

When you change routing or handoff behavior, update runbooks for support and operations teams so they know how conversations should flow and how to override AI when necessary.

</Callout>
